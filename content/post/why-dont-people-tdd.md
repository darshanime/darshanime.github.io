At the Go-Jek bootcamp, we have been thinking a lot about test driven development and how it leads to fewer bugs and more reliable software. Moreover, it also leads to a higher speed of development. A [recent study](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/blog/exploding-software-engineering-myths/?from=http%3A%2F%2Fresearch.microsoft.com%2Fen-us%2Fnews%2Ffeatures%2Fnagappan-100609.aspx ) from Microsoft concluded:

> Test-driven development has been shown to reduce defect rates 40% to 90%, with an increase in development time in the 15%-35% range.

The same message more or less has also been preached by Ajey Gore, CTO at Go-Jek. [Here](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FA0mmD0Y8cI) he is talking about how TDD helps better software, faster. 

Given all the benefits, I was wondering why it isn't as prevalent as it should be. In my previous working experience of 2 years, I had never come across any developer who was using this technique to write code. I too never bothered about it and considered it to be not worth the trouble. 

However, I tried it recently when writing Conway's Game Of Life in Ruby, a language I had never written in before and I was surprised at how efficient it made the entire process. Once I had the test pinned down, I was confident, positive even about writing the functionality, and once all was green, I could just continue building on from there, with confidence.
