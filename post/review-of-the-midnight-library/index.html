Review of The Midnight Library

April 29, 2021

The modern novel is dead. I spent the entire day yesterday reading what was supposed to be wonderful work of fiction by the acclaimed Matt Haig. The enterprise was bound to succeed. An acclaimed British author writes a novel which is published by a major publisher, has relentless marketing done on multiple media, is on the bestsellers list of many list makers and has even won the Goodreads Choice 2020 award. I freed up my Sunday and was all set to dive deep into the The Midnight Library. Only to come back disillusioned a few short pages later.

The writing was banal and repetitive. I like my fiction to be somewhat flowery, and The Midnight Library is the tritest possible while still making grammar sense.

Hi Izzy, long time no chat. Miss you, friend. Would be WONDEROUS to catch up. X
She added another ‘X’ and sent it.

Yawn.

Almost on cue, as if bad writing was not sufficient, there is the added mumbo-jumbo of “quantum physics”. The author confidently writes the most incredulous things without batting an eye.

Going for a coffee with Ash might have led, for instance, to Nora falling in love with the person serving the coffee. That was simply the unpredictable nature of quantum physics.

This is A grade. Cannot get better than this, enough to put Deepak Chopra to shame.

I persisted though, not giving up now that I had started. To tolerate the writing, I purchased the audio book and used that to keep myself reading despite myself.

In the first dozen pages, the story was laid out in its entirety. There is our protagonist Nora Seed, who is a wannabe Sylvia Plath. Nora is upset that she has made bad decisions in her life and has nothing to live for now. What bad decisions? She quit swimming which she hated, quit her brother’s rock band which she didn’t like being a part of anway, did not follow her friend Izzy to Australia and did not become a glaciologist to save the planet. Instead, she is a saleswoman at the local music store, which is called String Theory - the author leaves no stone unturned to show us he knows his Physics. After committing suicide, she is transported to a library that the author suggests poetically “is between death and life”.

Now, the writer goes into the deep end with his spooky science. Each decision in her life is a turning point and in some universe, she made a different decision and life turned out differently. She can live in that universe now if she so chooses. Nora of course does. What follows next is plan “make Nora suck in all universes, but also make her explore a gazillion universes so the book has some length”. I cannot believe I kept at it and read thru all the infinite repeats of the same cycle, all with some of the worst dialogues and goofy science sprinkled around.

When I was finally done in the end, I felt deeply cheated. Goodreads, audible, booksellers, everyone on twitter had conspired to make me read this book. I read this over Rushdie’s Midnight’s Children, Tolstoy’s War and Peace, James Joyce’s Ulysses. Going forward, I am not going to take this risk and won’t read any book on a whim.

Thanks Matt.
