So. The very first entry. 

How will the future play out ? Will this blog stay with me till I grow old ? Will it live beyond me to tell passerbys about a boy who once lived? Will it tell them of my triumphs and failures, motivations and fears, friends and foes or will I stop writing after a few weeks ?!  

Whatever be it, it has been fun orchestrating the various libraries, frameworks, repos that went into the making of this blog. 
All along the way, there was one emotion that ran like current through me; the feeling of *awe*. 

There are people who spit blood writing code that allows mortals like us make blogs with mere clicks. Hardwork screams from each feature of their work. Things that we take for granted are often sitting on huge mountains of complexity. Take the CSS code of this blog for example. Making this beauty took 1643 lines of SASS formatting. This is not your typical 

{% highlight python %}
print "Hello World !" 
{% endhighlight %}


This is mind boggling, heart wrenching 1643 lines of SASS. 

{% highlight SASS %}
h1, .h1, .f1 {
  font-size: 2rem;
  line-height: 2.5rem;
}

h2, .h2, .f2 {
  font-size: 1.5rem;
  line-height: 2rem;
}
{% endhighlight %}

Fine detail about how each pixel will be behave. 

Often, all this countless hours of work is then rolled into a repository and thrown to the public !
And hold on if you are already impressed, for that SASS code is just the tip of the iceberg. Entire libraries like [animate.css](https://github.com/daneden/animate.css) which animates this blog, to engines like [Jekyll](https://jekyllrb.com) which powers this blog, to entire languages like [Ruby](https://www.ruby-lang.org/en/) which power Jekyll are open source. They are available for everyone to see, learn from, change, use, distribute. **All for free**.

 
Amazing no ? <br>
This raises one important question. Is this the way it actually should be ?

I once attended a talk by a computer scientist who came to BITS. After he was finished, a friend of mine stood up. 

<blockquote>
  <p>
    How ethical is it to charge for a piece of code ?
  </p>
  <footer><cite title="Nice question SS :)">Sagar Shah</cite></footer>
</blockquote>

While the scientist was fumbling for an answer, I was thinking. Hard. 

There are people who hide their code behind sophisticated security layers and bind users with incomprehensible terms & conditions and charge money to access their services. Maybe it is *not* wrong. 

Then there are others who begin their license with the words 
>Permission is hereby granted, free of charge...


Who say you are free to do whatever you can think of doing with this set of computer instructions. You are free to even make money out of it.

Both sides are just in their thinking. We need both. We need robust proprietary as well as invaluable free code. 

One side charges for it's toil, just like in any other profession. We don't question the doctor's morality when he asks for consulting fees. So then, how can we be different to the programmer ?

On the other side, people who release their code believe that there are greater things than money in life. Things money can't buy. Like awe. They in their small way support the idea of free knowledge for all.

If the world is moving towards the latter is to be seen. I hope it is.
